The Evolving Landscape of AI and Copyright

A legal analysis of authorship, fair use, and cultural heritage in the age of generative AI

By Kudakwashe Kwitireni • Published 10 June 2025 • 12 min read

The rapid evolution of generative AI has ignited a fervent debate surrounding its intersection with intellectual property rights. Last month, I had the privilege of participating in a moot court competition hosted by the World Intellectual Property Organization (WIPO), where we delved into the complex issue of whether the utilization of generative AI, trained on datasets containing cultural heritage artifacts, to generate NFTs constitutes copyright infringement.

This essay serves as an analysis of this critical question, examining the potential infringement of copyright in AI-generated images. By exploring the nuances of this emerging legal landscape, I aim to contribute to the ongoing discourse and offer insights for policymakers, legal professionals, and creators alike.

The Nature of AI-Generated Content and Copyright Law

Copyright Law and Originality

Copyright law protects original works of authorship fixed in a tangible medium of expression. To be eligible for copyright protection, a work must exhibit a modicum of creativity and originality. The fundamental question arises: can AI-generated content meet these traditional standards of authorship?

The challenge lies in the fact that traditional copyright law was designed with human creators in mind. The concept of "authorship" has always been intrinsically linked to human creativity, intentionality, and expression. AI systems, regardless of their sophistication, operate through algorithmic processes and statistical patterns rather than conscious creative decisions.

The Role of Human Intervention

A key question in determining copyright ownership of AI-generated content is the extent of human intervention in the creative process. Courts and legal scholars are increasingly examining whether the human input rises to the level of authorship required for copyright protection.

If a human author provides substantial creative input—such as carefully crafted prompts, curated training data, or significant post-generation editing—they may be considered the author of the work. However, if the AI model generates content autonomously with minimal human guidance, establishing clear authorship becomes considerably more challenging.

This distinction has profound implications for copyright ownership, licensing, and commercial exploitation of AI-generated works.

AI and Copyright Infringement

Training Data and Copyright Infringement

One of the primary concerns regarding AI-generated content is the potential for copyright infringement during the training process. AI models are often trained on vast datasets that may include copyrighted works used without explicit authorization from rights holders.

This situation bears resemblance to the unauthorized use of copyrighted material examined in Harper & Row Publishers, Inc. v. Nation Enterprises, where the Supreme Court emphasized the importance of considering the purpose and character of the use, the nature of the copyrighted work, and the effect on the market for the original work.

The key legal question becomes: does the use of copyrighted works in AI training constitute fair use, or does it require explicit licensing from rights holders?

Fair Use and AI-Generated Content

The fair use doctrine, which allows for limited use of copyrighted material without permission under certain circumstances, may provide a defense against copyright infringement claims in AI contexts. However, the application of fair use to AI is complex and continues to evolve through litigation and scholarly debate.

Transformative Use

If AI-generated content significantly transforms the original copyrighted work—adding new meaning, purpose, or character—it may be more likely to qualify as fair use. This concept finds parallels in Google LLC v. Oracle America, Inc., where the Supreme Court recognized that copying software code could be transformative when used for a different purpose.

The challenge lies in determining when AI-generated output constitutes sufficient transformation of the original training data to warrant fair use protection.

Nature of the Copyrighted Work

The nature of the copyrighted work—whether it is factual or creative, published or unpublished—significantly influences the fair use analysis. Creative works typically receive stronger copyright protection, making fair use claims more difficult to establish.

Amount and Substantiality of the Portion Used

While AI models may process entire copyrighted works during training, the question becomes whether the resulting AI-generated content incorporates substantial portions of any specific copyrighted work. This analysis requires sophisticated technical and legal examination of how AI models store, process, and utilize training data.

Effect on the Market for the Original Work

The potential impact of AI-generated content on the market for original works represents a crucial factor in fair use analysis. If AI-generated works substitute for or diminish the market value of original copyrighted works, fair use protection becomes less likely.

Cultural Heritage and Copyright

Cultural heritage artifacts present unique challenges in the AI copyright context. Many such artifacts may be in the public domain due to age, but others remain under copyright protection. Additionally, cultural heritage raises questions about collective ownership, traditional knowledge, and the rights of indigenous communities.

Balancing Innovation and Protection

While AI has tremendous potential to democratize access to and appreciation of cultural heritage, we must carefully balance innovation with the protection of copyright and cultural rights. This balance requires consideration of several key factors:

The nature of the cultural heritage artifact: Is it a creative work subject to copyright protection, or is it in the public domain? Does it involve traditional knowledge or cultural expressions that may be protected under different legal frameworks?

The extent of AI incorporation: Does the AI model simply mimic the style of the artifact, or does it create something entirely new? How much of the original work is recognizable in the AI-generated output?

Market impact: Does the AI-generated content compete with or diminish the value of the original work? Does it create new markets or enhance appreciation for the original?

Platform Liability and AI-Generated Content

Platforms that host or distribute AI-generated content face complex liability questions. The Digital Millennium Copyright Act (DMCA) provides safe harbor protections for online service providers, but the application of these protections to AI-generated content remains uncertain.

The precedent established in A&M Records v. Napster demonstrates that platforms can be held liable for contributory and vicarious infringement if they have knowledge of infringing activities and benefit financially from such activities while failing to take action.

YouTube's Content ID system exemplifies how platforms attempt to balance copyright protection with user-generated content. However, applying such systems to AI-generated content presents unique technical and legal challenges, particularly in determining whether content is original, derivative, or infringing.

The Future of AI and Copyright

As AI technology continues to evolve at unprecedented speed, developing a comprehensive legal framework becomes increasingly urgent. Several key areas require attention:

International Cooperation

International cooperation is essential to harmonize copyright laws and ensure consistent protection of intellectual property rights across jurisdictions. The global nature of AI development and deployment demands coordinated international responses.

Technological Solutions

Technological measures, such as digital watermarking, blockchain-based provenance tracking, and advanced fingerprinting systems, can help identify and track the origin of AI-generated content, facilitating rights management and enforcement.

Licensing and Contractual Frameworks

Clear licensing agreements and contractual frameworks can help govern the use of copyrighted material in AI training and the subsequent distribution of AI-generated content. Industry-wide standards and best practices are beginning to emerge.

The Question of AI Authorship

The fundamental question of whether AI can be considered an author remains contentious. While AI systems can generate creative content, they lack the human capacity for independent thought, judgment, and intentional expression that traditionally underpins copyright protection.

Some jurisdictions are beginning to address this question legislatively. The UK's Intellectual Property Office has suggested that AI-generated works could be protected for a limited period, while other countries maintain that only human-authored works qualify for copyright protection.

Implications for Legal Practice

For legal practitioners, the intersection of AI and copyright presents both opportunities and challenges. Lawyers must now consider:

Due diligence in AI use: Understanding how AI tools process and utilize existing copyrighted works in their outputs.

Client counseling: Advising clients on the risks and benefits of using AI-generated content in their businesses and creative endeavors.

Contract drafting: Developing new contractual frameworks that address AI-generated content ownership, licensing, and liability.

Litigation strategy: Preparing for novel legal arguments in AI-related copyright disputes.

Recommendations for Policymakers

Based on this analysis, several recommendations emerge for policymakers grappling with AI and copyright:

Clarify fair use standards: Develop clear guidelines for when AI training and output generation constitute fair use of copyrighted materials.

Address authorship questions: Establish clear rules regarding AI authorship and the role of human creativity in AI-generated works.

Promote transparency: Require AI developers to provide transparency about their training data sources and methodologies.

Foster innovation: Ensure that copyright law evolves to promote rather than stifle beneficial AI applications while protecting legitimate rights holders.

Conclusion

The intersection of AI, cultural heritage, and copyright represents one of the most complex and rapidly evolving areas of intellectual property law. As we navigate this new frontier, we must balance the tremendous potential of AI to democratize creativity and enhance access to cultural heritage with the legitimate rights of creators and the integrity of our copyright system.

The legal profession has a crucial role to play in shaping this balance. By understanding the technical aspects of AI, engaging thoughtfully with policy questions, and developing innovative legal frameworks, we can help ensure that AI serves as a tool for human flourishing rather than a threat to creative expression.

The questions raised by AI and copyright are not merely academic—they will determine the future of creativity, innovation, and cultural preservation in the digital age. As future legal professionals, we must engage with these issues thoughtfully and proactively, helping to craft solutions that protect rights while fostering innovation.
