Introduction

At the heart of the legal profession and advocacy lies legal writing. Often, before you can present your arguments orally, you must first express them in writing In this sense, your writing skills are just as importantâ€”if not more soâ€”than your oral communication abilities. The way you organize your writing will determine whether a reader can understand and be persuaded by your argument. Flowery language, technical jargon, or brilliant rhetoric can only take you so far. If your work lacks a clear structure, many of your points will be lost or misunderstood.;

Most legal writing requires the writer to analyze a set of facts using legal principles drawn from a variety of sources, including statutes, judicial decisions, and secondary materials. Unlike the non-legal writing you may have encountered in high school or other fields, legal writing follows a specific structure that lawyers around the world useâ€”and expect to see in your work.

Whether it's called IRAC (Issue, Rule, Application, Conclusion), FIRAC (Facts, Issue, Rule, Application, Conclusion), CRAC (Conclusion, Rule, Application, Conclusion), or CREAC (Conclusion, Rule, Explanation, Application, Conclusion), the fundamental logic remains the same: state the issue, identify the applicable rule, apply that rule to the facts, and reach a conclusion

Although this approach may seem rigid or unfamiliar at first, it will quickly become second nature. This tutorial will walk you through how to structure a persuasive legal argument using the FIRAC method. The same core principles apply across its many variations

F â€“ Facts

The “F” in FIRAC stands for Facts. This is the starting point of any legal analysis The facts of a case form the factual foundation on which the legal issues rest. Not every detail is important: the skill lies in identifying which facts are legally relevantâ€”those that the court must consider in order to resolve the legal questionâ€”and which are irrelevant, background details that do not affect the legal outcome.

Relevant facts are those that trigger the application of a specific rule or law and frame the legal issue under dispute. Irrelevant facts may provide context or emotion, but they do not influence the legal reasoning.
