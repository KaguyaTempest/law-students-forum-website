Introduction

At the heart of the legal profession and advocacy lies legal writing. Often, before you can present your arguments orally, you must first express them in writing In this sense, your writing skills are just as important‚Äîif not more so‚Äîthan your oral communication abilities. The way you organize your writing will determine whether a reader can understand and be persuaded by your argument. Flowery language, technical jargon, or brilliant rhetoric can only take you so far. If your work lacks a clear structure, many of your points will be lost or misunderstood.;

Most legal writing requires the writer to analyze a set of facts using legal principles drawn from a variety of sources, including statutes, judicial decisions, and secondary materials. Unlike the non-legal writing you may have encountered in high school or other fields, legal writing follows a specific structure that lawyers around the world use‚Äîand expect to see in your work.

Whether it's called IRAC (Issue, Rule, Application, Conclusion), FIRAC (Facts, Issue, Rule, Application, Conclusion), CRAC (Conclusion, Rule, Application, Conclusion), or CREAC (Conclusion, Rule, Explanation, Application, Conclusion), the fundamental logic remains the same: state the issue, identify the applicable rule, apply that rule to the facts, and reach a conclusion

Although this approach may seem rigid or unfamiliar at first, it will quickly become second nature. This tutorial will walk you through how to structure a persuasive legal argument using the FIRAC method. The same core principles apply across its many variations

To illustrate how the FIRAC method can be applied in practice, we will examine the case of Magaya v Magaya. Using this case as a template, we will walk through each step of the FIRAC structure to analyse the legal reasoning and arrive at a conclusion‚Äîjust as the court did

Magaya v Magaya

Supreme Court of Zimbabwe ‚Äì Judgment No. S.C. 210/98 ‚Äì Decided 16 February 1999

Upon the death of Shoniwa Magaya in 1994, his daughter Venia Magaya was appointed heir to his estate by a magistrate‚Äôs court. Shortly afterward, her younger half-brother, Nhaka Magaya, contested her appointment and applied for it to be set aside on the grounds that under Shona customary law, a woman could not be appointed heir when a male heir existed

F ‚Äì Facts

The “F” in FIRAC stands for Facts. This is the starting point of any legal analysis The facts of a case form the factual foundation on which the legal issues rest. Not every detail is important: the skill lies in identifying which facts are legally relevant‚Äîthose that the court must consider in order to resolve the legal question‚Äîand which are irrelevant, background details that do not affect the legal outcome.

Relevant facts are those that trigger the application of a specific rule or law and frame the legal issue under dispute. Irrelevant facts may provide context or emotion, but they do not influence the legal reasoning.

How to identify relevant facts

When beginning a legal analysis ask yourself:

Would the court need this fact to decide the legal issue?

if the answer is yes, it‚Äôs a relevant fact. If not, set it aside. A focused factual summary allows you to concentrate on the rule and analysis that follow.

Applying it to Magaya v Magaya

  • Shoniwa Magaya died in 1994.
  • His daughter Venia Magaya was initially appointed heir to his estate by a magistrate‚Äôs court.
  • Her younger half-brother, Nhaka Magaya, contested the appointment based on Shona customary law.
  • The magistrate later reversed the decision, awarding heirship to Nhaka.
  • Venia appealed to the Supreme Court, arguing that disqualifying her based on sex was unconstitutional.

I ‚Äì Issue

The ‚ÄúI‚Äù in FIRAC stands for Issue. This is the legal question the court is asked to answer. A well-formulated issue frames the dispute in precise, legal terms. It connects the relevant facts to the applicable rule of law.

How to Identify the Issue

  • What specific legal question arises from these facts?
  • What is the court being asked to decide?

An effective legal issue is:

  • Specific (not vague or overly broad)
  • Legally framed (not just a factual dispute)
  • Often phrased as a question beginning with ‚ÄúWhether‚Ä¶‚Äù or ‚ÄúDoes‚Ä¶‚Äù

What does it mean for an issue to be "legally framed"?

When we say an issue must be legally framed, we mean that it must be written as a legal question ‚Äî not just a general or emotional question ‚Äî and it must refer to a specific law, right, or legal principle.

A legally framed issue connects the facts of the case to a specific rule of law, such as a constitutional right, a statute, or a court decision. It shows what the court needs to decide as a matter of law, not just as a matter of fairness or opinion.

Example:

  • ‚ùå Not legally framed: ‚ÄúIs it fair that Venia didn‚Äôt get the inheritance?‚Äù
  • ‚úîÔ∏è Legally framed: ‚ÄúDoes disqualifying Venia from inheriting under customary law violate her constitutional right to non-discrimination on the grounds of sex?‚Äù

Note: Avoid issues that are too general or that compound multiple legal questions into one. If there is more than one issue, break them up and handle them separately.

Applying it to Magaya v Magaya

From the relevant facts, the central legal issue is:

Whether the exclusion of a woman from inheritance under Shona customary law violates the constitutional protection against discrimination on the grounds of sex.
  • Focuses on the gender-based disqualification
  • Identifies the relevant source of law: the Constitution
  • Narrows the issue to what the court had to decide ‚Äî not whether the rule was fair or moral, but whether it was unconstitutional

Teaching Point:

  • Be legally precise
  • Use the relevant facts to frame the legal problem
  • Keep it neutral ‚Äî don‚Äôt assume the answer in the way you phrase the question

Remember: a clearly framed issue is the backbone of your legal argument. It guides what rules to apply, how to analyze them, and where to draw your conclusion.

R ‚Äì Rule

The ‚ÄúR‚Äù in FIRAC stands for Rule. This refers to the legal principles or laws that the court must use to resolve the issue. Your Rule section should resemble a funnel: begin with the broadest sources of law and narrow down to more specific provisions, exceptions, or cases. Generally, this means starting with constitutional or statutory provisions, followed by relevant case law and customary law, where applicable.

These rules can come from:

  • Statutes or legislation (e.g. the Constitution or Acts of Parliament)
  • Case law (decisions from previous courts)
  • Customary law (in matters such as inheritance, marriage, or guardianship)
  • Secondary sources (such as academic commentary, which are persuasive but not binding)
Teaching Point: Be Specific and Precise
  • Name the source (e.g. section 23 of the Constitution of Zimbabwe, 1980)
  • Quote or paraphrase the exact legal language
  • Explain its effect in the context of the issue

Avoid vague references like ‚Äúthe Constitution says...‚Äù ‚Äî pinpoint the exact clause or case and explain what it does.

When using case law:

  • Provide the full case name and proper citation (e.g. Magaya v Magaya S.C. 210/98)
  • Mention the court and year of decision
  • Paraphrase or quote the key legal principle the court established
  • Identify the judge (e.g. per Gubbay CJ) when it adds clarity or authority

‚úÖ Example of Good Rule Presentation:

Section 23(1) of the Constitution of Zimbabwe (1980) prohibits laws or actions that discriminate based on sex. However, section 23(3)(b) provides an exception: it permits the application of customary law in matters of personal law, including ‚Äúdevolution of property on death.‚Äù In Magaya v Magaya S.C. 210/98 (Supreme Court of Zimbabwe, 1999), the Court held that this exception allowed customary inheritance practices that disqualify women from heirship.

Rules Applied in Magaya v Magaya:

  1. Section 23 of the 1980 Constitution of Zimbabwe
    • Prohibited sex-based discrimination in general
    • Section 23(3) created an exception for discrimination arising from customary law in matters of personal law, including inheritance
  2. Shona Customary Law on Inheritance
    • Male descendants are customarily preferred over female descendants when determining heirs
  3. Administration of Estates Act [Chapter 6:01]
    • Estates of individuals governed by customary law are to be administered in accordance with that law

A ‚Äì Application

The ‚ÄúA‚Äù in FIRAC stands for Application, also referred to as Analysis. This is where you apply the rule(s) to the facts to demonstrate how the legal principles lead to a specific conclusion. It is the core of persuasive legal writing ‚Äî the section where you make your case.

üß† What Makes a Good Application?
  • Connects the rule to the relevant facts
  • Explains how and why the rule applies (or does not apply)
  • Follows logical progression ‚Äî each sentence builds on the last
  • Addresses counterarguments where appropriate
  • Uses analogies or distinctions with other cases to support reasoning

Avoid merely restating the facts or summarizing the rule. The Application section is where you show the court how the rule actually works in your case.

Applying it to Magaya v Magaya

In Magaya v Magaya, the Supreme Court applied the legal rule to the facts as follows:

  • Venia Magaya‚Äôs disqualification from heirship was based solely on her sex, which would ordinarily fall foul of section 23(1)‚Äôs prohibition on discrimination.
  • However, the Court highlighted that section 23(3) provides an exception: discrimination is not unconstitutional if it arises from the application of customary law in matters of personal law, such as inheritance.
  • Because Shona customary law gives preference to male heirs, and this area of law is protected by the exception in section 23(3), the Court held that there was no constitutional violation.
  • The judgment emphasized judicial restraint, stating that reforming customary norms falls under the role of the legislature, not the courts.
‚ÄúThe appellant, being a woman, was not entitled to inherit under customary law. That law, in the light of s 23(3), cannot be held to be unconstitutional.‚Äù
‚Äî per Gubbay CJ, Magaya v Magaya S.C. 210/98
Teaching Point: Reasoning, Not Repetition

Don‚Äôt just list facts or summarize rules. Your task is to explain how one leads to the other. Here‚Äôs the difference:

  • ‚ùå Weak application:
    ‚ÄúThe rule allows discrimination under customary law. Venia is a woman. The court applied the rule.‚Äù
  • ‚úÖ Effective application:
    ‚ÄúAlthough Venia‚Äôs disqualification was based on her sex ‚Äî something section 23(1) would normally prohibit ‚Äî the Court applied section 23(3), which expressly permits the use of customary inheritance rules. Because these rules favor male heirs, and the Constitution makes space for that exception, the Court concluded that the discrimination was constitutionally valid.‚Äù

Your Application = Your Argument

This is where you do your persuasive work. Whether you‚Äôre writing a legal memo, a moot court brief, or an exam answer, the Application section is your stage. It‚Äôs where you show your reasoning, judgment, and skill.

In this section, you can:

  • Highlight logical consequences of applying a rule a certain way
  • Use fairness, justice, or public policy to support your interpretation
  • Introduce emotionally compelling angles (like gender equity) ‚Äî as long as they are grounded in legal reasoning
  • Show how similar or different your case is from past judgments
  • Persuade the reader (or judge) that your interpretation of the law is more sound, just, or reasonable

Just remember: even your emotional or policy-based appeals must be anchored in law. They should reinforce your analysis ‚Äî not replace it.

C ‚Äì Conclusion

The ‚ÄúC‚Äù in FIRAC stands for Conclusion. This is the final step in your legal analysis, where you summarize your answer to the issue based on your application of the rule to the facts. It may be only a few sentences long, but it should be clear, decisive, and logically consistent with everything you‚Äôve written above.

What Makes a Strong Conclusion?

  • It directly answers the legal issue you identified earlier
  • It reflects the legal reasoning and structure of your Application section
  • It does not introduce new facts or rules ‚Äî it‚Äôs a resolution, not a new discussion
  • In argumentative writing (e.g., a moot or opinion), it can also reinforce your position or recommendation

Think of your conclusion as the judgment of your argument ‚Äî it‚Äôs where the judge (or reader) hears your final word on what should happen and why.

Conclusion in Magaya v Magaya

Based on the Court‚Äôs interpretation of section 23(3) of the Constitution, and the recognition of Shona customary inheritance rules as a valid exception, the Court held that Venia Magaya‚Äôs exclusion from heirship ‚Äî while discriminatory ‚Äî was not unconstitutional. The appeal was dismissed, and Nhaka Magaya remained the lawful heir to the estate.

A weak conclusion merely restates the issue or rewords the rule.

  • Weak:
    ‚ÄúSo, the Constitution allows customary law, and Venia was not the heir.‚Äù
  • Strong:
    ‚ÄúBecause section 23(3) allows customary rules to govern inheritance, and because those rules favored male heirs, the Court upheld the magistrate‚Äôs ruling in favor of Nhaka. Venia‚Äôs disqualification, while discriminatory, was legally valid under the Constitution.‚Äù

If you're writing an argument rather than a summary, this is also the place to:

  • State clearly which party should win and why
  • Make a final appeal to justice, principle, or policy, as long as it‚Äôs consistent with your legal reasoning
  • Leave the reader (or judge) with a sense of closure and conviction
